Fibrodysplasia Ossificans Progressiva

Some diseases don't announce themselves. This one builds a second skeleton in silence.

FOP turns muscle, tendon, and ligament into bone — quietly, permanently, one flare-up at a time. Most doctors will never see a case. That's exactly why awareness, not just medicine, is what protects the people who have it.

The Biology

A gene that never learns to stop

FOP is caused by a single misfire in a gene called ACVR1 — the gene responsible for telling the body where and when to build bone. In someone with FOP, that switch is stuck on. The body keeps receiving a signal to grow bone in places it never should: inside muscles, tendons, and ligaments.

Over years, this forms what patients and researchers call a "second skeleton" — bridges of bone growing across joints, slowly fusing the body into a fixed position. There is currently no way to reverse bone that has already formed.

"FOP is not just rare — it is routinely misunderstood, misdiagnosed, and accidentally made worse by the very people trying to help."

— Palak Rana Shahzad, "When Flesh Turns into Bone," The HEART Venture, 2026
Recognition Saves Mobility

The signs doctors miss — and families can catch

FOP leaves clues on the body from birth. Most are dismissed as minor. Knowing what to look for is currently one of the only tools available to prevent irreversible harm.

01

The bent big toe

Present at birth in nearly every FOP patient — the big toe is shorter than normal, often turned inward, sometimes missing a joint. It's the single most reliable early marker, and the most commonly dismissed.

02

Sudden soft-tissue swelling

Painful, warm lumps that appear on the neck, shoulders, back, or upper arms — often after a minor bump or illness. They look and feel like tumors, which is exactly why they're so often misdiagnosed as cancer.

03

A biopsy makes it worse

Cutting into a flare-up to test it doesn't diagnose FOP — it triggers new bone growth at the cut site. A joint that still moved before the procedure can be permanently locked afterward.

Why It Takes So Long

Years lost to the wrong diagnosis

BirthCorrect diagnosis: 4–5 yrs later

FOP rarely appears in standard medical training. With roughly one diagnosed case per two million people, most physicians will never encounter it in their entire career — so the burden of recognition falls on families who've never heard of it either. Every year of delay is a year where flare-ups get mismanaged and mobility is lost for good.

Where Medicine Stands Today

There is still no cure. But there is, for the first time, a treatment.

Emergency response

Corticosteroids, within 24 hours

If given in the first day of a flare-up, steroids can reduce swelling and limit bone formation — but most patients are undiagnosed and miss this window entirely.

What to avoid

Surgery, biopsies, injections

Any invasive procedure — even routine dental work — can trigger new, permanent bone growth at the site. Many patients lose mobility not from the disease alone, but from well-meaning treatment.

2022 – 2023

Palovarotene (Sohonos) approved

Approved by Health Canada in January 2022 and the FDA in August 2023, it's the first-ever authorized treatment for FOP. It doesn't cure the disease, but it slows new bone formation during flare-ups — the first real medical tool patients have had.
